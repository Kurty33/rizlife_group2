Key Themes in Rizal’s Works

Social Injustice

Rizal pointed out the deep social inequalities in the Philippines under Spanish rule, and Majul points out how such themes continue to resonate today. The stark differences between the elite and the marginalized, which El Filibusterismo was focused on, still exist in the modern societies, including the Philippines. (Majul, 1999)

Political Corruption

Rizal's exposure of political corruption in the novel remains relevant, especially in nations grappling with political dysfunction and the abuse of power. Rizal's portrayal of figures like the Captain-General reveals how political elites manipulate the system for personal gain and maintain power at the expense of the masses that in the present day, corrupts are still presents globally.

Struggle for Freedom

relevant to modern-day fights for political autonomy and social rights. Rizal's characters, especially Simoun (Ibarra), are used as the setting for the fight against oppressive systems; it is his vision for a free and just society that continues to inspire modern movements. Rizal's work underlines the need to be in opposition to tyranny, a message which is very important even into the 21st century.

The Importance of Education

Education is central to El Filibusterismo, seen as a tool for personal and societal liberation. Rizal highlights how the elite monopolize knowledge, leaving the masses in ignorance. Through characters like Isagani, Rizal stresses that education should foster critical thinking, national consciousness, and social responsibility, empowering Filipinos to challenge injustice and build a just society.

The Call for Social Reform

Rizal critiques a society marked by deep inequality, where the elite, aligned with colonial powers, oppress the poor. Through Simoun, Rizal explores the dangers of vengeance-driven revolution, advocating for thoughtful, systemic reform over chaotic rebellion. True progress comes from reforming institutions, not simply overthrowing them.

Critical Thinking

and Social Action

El Filibusterismo remains a powerful catalyst for critical thinking and social activism. By exposing the consequences of systemic corruption, oppression, and ignorance, the novel encourages readers to question authority, challenge injustices, and advocate for meaningful change.

The Pursuit of True Freedom

True freedom is portrayed as living in a society that respects individual dignity and rights. Rizal critiques the colonial government's superficial freedom, which maintained their control. True freedom, for Rizal, involves moral awakening, social justice, and collective action, not just political independence or violent rebellion.

Education as a

Tool for Change

The novel highlights how education can be both a weapon and a means of oppression. While the students in the novel seek reforms through knowledge, the ruling elite use education to control and manipulate the people. Today, education remains a key factor in empowering individuals and challenging societal injustices.
