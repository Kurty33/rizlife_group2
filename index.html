Introduction

El Filibusterismo is a novel written by José Rizal and published in 1891. It serves as a sequel to *Noli Me Tangere*, continuing the narrative of Filipino struggles under Spanish rule. The novel reflects the deep social injustices and oppression experienced by Filipinos during the colonial period.

Unlike *Noli Me Tangere*, which exposes societal issues in a reformist manner, *El Filibusterismo* takes on a darker tone. The protagonist, Simoun, is a mysterious, wealthy jeweler who seeks to incite revolution rather than advocate for peaceful change. His character embodies the growing resentment and radical approach towards freedom.

Rizal condemns the corrupt institutions of the time, particularly the Spanish government and clergy. The novel serves as a wake-up call for Filipinos to recognize the need for reform and independence. Its themes of revolution, justice, and sacrifice continue to inspire discussions on nationalism and social change.

Biography

José Rizal (1861–1896) was a Filipino nationalist, physician, and writer who inspired the Philippine nationalist movement. He went to school in Manila and later in Madrid, where he advocated reform under Spanish rule without seeking independence.

His two novels, *Noli Me Tangere* (1887) and *El Filibusterismo* (1891), exposed Spanish abuses, while working on *Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas* (1890) highlighted the pre-colonial history of the Philippines.

Political Advocacy & Exile

As the leader of the Propaganda Movement, Rizal advocated for Filipino rights, including representation in the Spanish Cortes and equality before the law.

In 1892, after founding La Liga Filipina, he was exiled to Dapitan. Following the Katipunan uprising in 1896, he was arrested and executed for sedition.

His martyrdom ignited a stronger push for Philippine independence, and his farewell poem, Mi Último Adiós, became an enduring symbol of his legacy.

Legacy and Martyrdom

In 1895, Rizal asked for permission to travel to Cuba as an army doctor. His request was approved, but in August 1896, Katipunan, a nationalist Filipino society founded by Andres Bonifacio, revolted.

Rizal was convicted of sedition and sentenced to death by firing squad. Rizal's public execution was carried out in Manila on December 30, 1896, when he was 35 years old. His execution created more opposition to Spanish rule.

His martyrdom ignited a stronger push for Philippine independence, and his farewell poem, Mi Último Adiós, became an enduring symbol of his legacy.
